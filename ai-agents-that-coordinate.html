AI agents that actually work together — without the setup

"Multi-agent AI" is having a moment. The promise is real: instead of one chatbot answering everything, a team of specialist agents divides the work and coordinates. The problem is that, until recently, getting it meant a developer weekend — a spare machine, a terminal, a Discord bridge, and a stack of config files. Most people who'd benefit most never make it past step one.

What "agents that work together" actually means

A single chatbot answers alone. It can't ask a colleague, and it forgets your business between chats. Coordinated agents are different: each is a specialist, and they hand work to each other.

You ask: "Plan a campaign for the client who bills us the most."
The Marketing agent asks Accounting which client that is (and the margin), asks Research about their competitors, then returns one plan — built from numbers it never made you paste in.

That hand-off is the whole point. It's also the thing a single model — however good — structurally cannot do.

The DIY route, and why it's not for everyone

Open-source agent frameworks can do this, and they're impressive. But the honest setup checklist looks like: a dedicated Mac or always-on server, comfort in the terminal, API keys wired by hand, a chat bridge to talk to it, and ongoing maintenance when something breaks. That's a fine project for an engineer. It's a non-starter for a consultant, an agency owner, or anyone whose job isn't DevOps.
